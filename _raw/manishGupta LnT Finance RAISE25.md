## AlphaFold and AI for science

- **Nobel Prizes to computer scientists**  
He explains that a Nobel Prize in Chemistry and one in Physics recently went to computer scientists, predicting that this trend will continue as AI drives scientific breakthroughs.  
[Watch from 1:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=94s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **What is protein folding?**  
He revisits high‑school biology: proteins are chains of amino acids that fold into unique 3D shapes, and predicting that shape from the amino acid sequence has been a grand scientific challenge.  
[Watch from 1:56](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=116s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **CASP competition and AlphaFold’s breakthrough**  
He describes the CASP protein structure prediction competition and how AlphaFold, using transformers and reinforcement learning, first became number one and then, in 2020, matched experimental ground truth so closely that the community considered the problem essentially solved.  
[Watch from 2:59](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=179s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Scaling impact: 200 million proteins and “billion PhD years”**  
DeepMind released predicted structures for around 200 million proteins, compressing an estimated “billion PhD years” of work into less than a year, turning 3–5 years of work per protein into seconds.  
[Watch from 4:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=240s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Downstream applications of AlphaFold**  
He notes wide use by over two million researchers globally, including 150,000 in India, in areas like drug discovery (designing molecules that bind to targets) and plastic pollution (enzymes to degrade plastics).  
[Watch from 4:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=279s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **“Root node problems”**  
Co‑founder Demis Hassabis calls protein folding a “root node problem,” where solving a fundamental challenge unlocks solutions for many other problems.  
[Watch from 5:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=324s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

## From deep learning to foundation models

- **Deep learning revival**  
He reviews the revival of deep learning about 15 years ago, where simple mathematical units (“neurons”) stacked in many layers achieved breakthroughs in image classification and then across domains like speech recognition and machine translation.  
[Watch from 7:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=470s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Limitations: task‑specific training and labeled data**  
He points out two problems: each task needed a new network trained from scratch, and success relied on huge labeled datasets (millions of images, hours of audio, or sentence pairs), which is impractical for many business problems.  
[Watch from 9:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=590s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

## Transformers, BERT and language understanding

- **Transformers as an enabler**  
He explains that the transformer architecture, invented at Google Research, enabled a new wave of **foundation models** that could be pre‑trained on large corpora and reused across tasks.  
[Watch from 10:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=629s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **BERT and masked language modeling**  
He describes BERT: trained on text from sources like Wikipedia, it randomly masks about 15 percent of words and learns to predict them from context, similar to school “fill in the blanks” exercises.  
[Watch from 10:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=647s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Learning language and context**  
By predicting masked words such as “born” from context like “Obama 1961 Hawaii,” BERT learns rich representations that transfer to tasks like sentiment analysis and summarization without task‑specific data.  
[Watch from 11:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=676s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

## PaLM, foundation models and GPT vs Gemini

- **PaLM vs 58 task‑specific models**  
He presents Google’s PaLM model, evaluated on 58 different tasks against the then best specialized model for each; PaLM outperformed most of them, demonstrating the power of a single foundation model.  
[Watch from 12:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=765s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Coining “foundation models”**  
He notes that Stanford researchers coined the term “foundation models” to describe such pre‑trained models that provide a strong base for many downstream tasks.  
[Watch from 13:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=804s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Safety concerns and GPT’s early launch**  
Google hesitated to launch products based on PaLM due to concerns like hallucinations and bias, while OpenAI released GPT despite similar issues, showing that users still derived significant value.  
[Watch from 13:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=823s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Gemini as a unified model**  
This led to launching Gemini, a general‑purpose model that increasingly powers many Google products, moving beyond pure “large language model” framing into broader multimodal capabilities.

## Gemini 1.5: long-context multimodal AI

## Efficient LLMs and Matrioska transformers

- **Energy and cost of LLMs**  
He highlights rising energy demands of LLMs, mentioning large capital expenditures (tens of billions of dollars per year) on AI hardware, and frames efficiency as a multi‑billion‑dollar problem.  
[Watch from 33:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=2030s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Matrioska transformers concept**  
He introduces “Matrioska transformers” (MatFormer), named after Russian nested dolls, where a 16‑billion‑parameter model contains nested 8B, 4B, 2B and 1B models that each perform competitively with standalone models of the same size.  
[Watch from 34:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=2071s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Dynamic routing based on query complexity**  
Depending on query complexity, the system can route to a smaller or larger sub‑model, for example using a small model to answer “Who is the prime minister of India?” and reserving the largest model for complex reasoning, saving energy.  
[Watch from 35:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=2130s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Deployment on mobile (Gemma 3N)**  
He says MatFormer is used in Gemma 3N models on mobile phones, where only one model can be stored but multiple nested capabilities are effectively available.  
[Watch from 35:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=2130s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)

- **Systematic optimizations and impact**  
The team examined different LLM components (layers, generation, attention, feed‑forward, softmax) and introduced many optimizations that yield 20 percent, 40 percent, 2x or even 4x efficiency improvements, translating to multi‑billion‑dollar savings and lower overall energy consumption.  
[Watch from 35:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug&t=2172s) [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YN4GqiHryug)
