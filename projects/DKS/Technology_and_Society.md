# 🌐 Technology & Society

Critical analysis of India's technological adaptation and future challenges.

### Infrastructure: Water & Energy

**Post 410 (09/07/24)**
Bangalore city got its piped water supply in 1896 for the first time... Sir MV didn't expect Bangalore to grow... Still, he chaired a committee to decide on a source of water... It could meet our water requirements till the late fifties.

**Post 411 (10/07/24)**
The major problem was that Bangalore is more than 400 meters above the level of Cauvery river. So a massive quantity of water has to be pushed up to a height of more than 1000 and this required a lot of electrical energy... Fortunately Karnataka constructed the massive 1000 MW capacity Sharavathi valley hydroelectric project... KPC [Karnataka Power Corporation] was handling transmission and distribution.

**Post 412 (11/07/24)**
Today we are drawing 1450 MLD of water from cauvery. Still it is 30% less than the demand... pilferage is estimated at 30%... Our policy and approach considered only the supply side... It didn't look at the demand minimization.

**Post 414 (14/07/24)**
Traditionally the city used rain water... Rain water collection... raja kaluve which drained the water into the lakes... Unfortunately, this system has been destroyed... raja kaluve had also been encroached.

### The PC Revolution & Microprocessors

**Post 431 (30/07/24)**
A technological explosion was happening quietly... The arrival of IBM PC. It reduced the cost of a computer by several orders of magnitude - from crores of rupees to a lakh... The first generation computers used vacuum tubes... second generation used transistors... then came integrated circuits... then very large integration (VLSI).

**Post 431 (Cont.)**
Intel produced the first 4 bits processor in 1971... Intel went into designing and producing new processors starting with a popular 8 bits 8085 and then moving on to 16 bits processors 8086, 80186, 80286, 80386... I built a desktop with a 80286 processor and used it for more than 15 years.

**Post 434 (02/08/24)**
Mainframes concentrated on building a complex working system with better performances... PCs used packaged VLSI components. Monolith and big companies like IBM lost this game.

**Post 435 (03/08/24)**
The porting of Unix in the higher end PCs in our companies like HCL, Wipro, PSI... was done by a group of iisc alumni engineers - Mahesh Jain, Gopi and Ravi - who later started a very successful company called Integra micro systems in bangalore... It is the front runner in fintech.

### The IISc 66kV Power Project (1985-1986)

**Post 461 (29/08/24)**
In 1985, IISc was in a similar situation... As the chair of the power committee... I was concerned about the growth of our power demands. I talked to Prof CNR Rao... He understood my statements and agreed immediately... I decided on 66kv supply to IISc from KEB.

**Post 461 (Cont.)**
I met with problems from within and without. Prof Chandrasekariah [HV department] suggested three overhead 11kv lines instead... cost was less but interruptions would be high. I rejected his suggestion. The second objection was from the chairman of KEB, Mr BG Rudrappa. He told Prof CNR Rao that IISc didn't need 66kv... I gave only one fact: “Do you want iisc to be one in thousands for it's power supply or one in ten.” The answer was in favor of 66kv.

**Post 462 (30/08/24)**
...We decided to go for Raychem jointing for 11 kv cables... The total cost estimate was about 1.5 crore rupees... I was not prepared to experiment with inexperienced companies. Sympathy won't work here. Bombay Suburban was the lowest tender.

**Post 463 (31/08/24)**
...I kept the cable component outside the main tender... expensive... manufacturers could ensure quality. A new company from Kochi, Premier Cables, quoted less than 50 lakhs (others quoted over 1 crore). We decided to see the production facility... We saved about fifty lakhs rupees. They produced 1.2 km in one run without break. One joint was needed at the entry point; we got Westinghouse engineers flown in to do the precast joint.

I was made a member of both [Karnataka and Kerala] states' task forces. I visited most engineering colleges in Karnataka and Kerala. **Post 475 (12/09/24)**

### The Three Eras of Educational Tools

**Post 491 (28/09/24)**
To understand the usage of tools in India, I divide the period 1960 to 2024 into three eras.

**Era 1: 1960–1980 (Pre-Digital Era)**
We had great class rooms with huge glass boards... IISc had many good labs... a central workshop with more machines than a machine tool industry. But most teachers had limited knowledge... they accessed research papers only after two to three years. We had an ammonia printing unit, an epidiascope, and a cyclostyling machine. We had analog computers.

**Era 2: 1980–2000 (Digital Growth Era)**
Microprocessors, PCs, servers, software, and networks. Internationally students used PCs effectively... but the situation in India was different. We concentrated on teaching hardware (8085). By the 90s, C became usable along with Unix... Graduates became available for industries like TCS. Tools for teachers were mainly the overhead projector (OHP) and transparency sheets. ERNET was a great infrastructure for email communications.

### Hardware Sizing and the Banking Revolution

**Post 523 (30/10/24)**
The banks started branch automation based on the Rangarajan committee report during the late eighties... I felt that this partial automation will not be beneficial, total bank automation was needed with a centralised database - that is the key.

**Post 523 (Cont.) - Sizing Methodology**
Most people even now have no idea of the sizing methodology. Hardware sizing depends on:
1. Operating systems and applications software features
2. Workload (tps)
3. Software Capability on throughputs
4. Concurrency levels
5. 24x7 availability
6. Data management capabilities
7. Latencies
8. Communications delays
9. Business continuity
10. Types of machines available.
Normally sizing is done through simulations of increasing workloads.

**Post 526 (02/11/24)**
When PNB wanted to bring all its branches into core banking, they had discussions with Infosys. Infosys wasn't sure... After a strong stance by the CMD to Nandan, he agreed. So we insisted on a stress test. It was done in England.

### The Evolution of Engineering: 20th vs 21st Century

**Post 549 (25/11/24)**
Engineering is about changes and transformations. Its inventions affect the people directly and indirectly, but we ignored the negative aspects... Today's focus is people orientation not product or process or success orientation. Success doesn't depend on your plant, but on supply chain, sales and service and most importantly customer satisfaction.

**Post 550 (26/11/24)**

- **20th Century**: Focused on industry applications, functional specs, central enterprise software, central IT departments.
- **21st Century**: People-first, SMAC (Social, Mobile, Analytics, Cloud), decentralised control, agile, horizontal organizations, customer-first involvement.
IT moved very fast from air-conditioned offices to our pockets. We need to build a curriculum based on "Engineering for Humans".

**Post 552 (28/11/24)**

### Infrastructure and Reform: The CET Experiment

We still have strong PCs [in 1994], but less powerful servers. Dial up was the only way to connect a PC to a communications network... The government created the CET cell, a unit to conduct engineering admissions... a major transformation... It was decided to make admissions by a completely computerised system at a single point.

### Engineering in the 21st Century: The Transformation

**Post 575 (21/12/24) - Shifts in Principles**
We shifted from job focus to skills focus, from single skills to multiskills.
- **Organization**: Monolithic, vertical, enterprise-first (20th) vs. Startup culture, horizontal, flexible, distributed (21st).
- **Control**: Centralised (20th) vs. Empowered teams (21st).
- **Objective**: Product/Process focus (20th) vs. People-centric (21st).
- **Strategy**: Structured, risk-averse (20th) vs. Agile, experimentation, disruptive innovation (21st).
- **Core Driver**: Functions (20th) vs. Data (21st).

**Post 576 (22/12/24) - The VUCA World**
1. **Volatile**: Systems should use agile, flexible processes. Need adaptation for survival.
2. **Uncertain**: Randomness; statistics play a big role, not just deterministic mathematics.
3. **Complex and Dynamic**: Glocal thinking. Architecting an engineering system becomes a critical activity. Move from problem solving in the small to problem solving in the large.
4. **Ambiguous**: Need futuristic vision and multiple solutions.

### Redefining Education for the 21st Century

**Post 644 (06/03/25) - The Colonial Legacy**
The current education system is still Macaulay-based, producing clerks. It ignores innovation and forbids querying. Rote learning is preferred. This system destroyed our heritage and made us feel inferior. We need to tell people about the extensive knowledge repository in our Upanishads, Sastras, and grammar systems.

**Post 647 (09/03/25) - Publication vs. Patent**
A paper-to-patent ratio of one is a disaster for research.
- **Publications**: Two centuries old, accepted norm, peer interactions, cross-fertilization of ideas. Most technical advances come from publications (e.g., computers).
- **Patents**: Output of R&D, commercial role. But patents often lead to *jugaad* (small innovations), not breakthroughs. Breakthroughs are often rejected by patent offices.
- **IISc Practice**: Measure an academic multidimensionally—publications, projects, consultancies, teaching. All people shouldn't be measured on one yardstick.

**Post 648 (10/03/25) - NEP and Hurdles**
The New Education Policy (NEP) addresses concerns on holistic, enquiry-based education. But implementation is embroiled in controversies—politicalization, rigid views, and "one size fits all" solutions. We need locally tailored, locally relevant, and locally meaningful solutions.

**Post 651 (13/03/25) - A Proposed School Curriculum**
1. **Life and Living**: Who am I? Family, society, ethics, good habits, conservation.
2. **Nature**: Water sources, trees, soil, ecology. Relationship of nature to humans.
3. **Human Body**: Functions, health, yoga, meditation, concentration.
4. **Essentials for Survival**: Basic math (arithmetic, geometry), science (energy, reactions), technology (transport, electricity).
5. **Society**: Governance, culture, geography.

**Post 652 (14/03/25) - Humanizing Education**
We should emphasize lives of leaders (Gandhi, Vinobha), scientists (CV Raman, JC Bose), and engineers (Visvesvarya, Dhawan). Education should be founded on Gandhi's principles:
- **Truth (Satya)** and **Non-violence (Ahimsa)**.
- **Satyagraha**: Moral persuasion over coercion.
- **Seven Social Sins**: Wealth without work, Pleasure without conscience, Knowledge without character, Commerce without morality, Science without humaneness, Religion without sacrifice, Politics without principles.

### The Architecture of Thinking and Learning

**Post 664 (26/03/25) - Reflective Thinking**
Thinking is the main objective of our education system. John Dewey defined "reflective thinking" as the active, persistent, and careful consideration of a belief in light of the grounds that support it. Curiosity and questioning are integral. Formal education teaches mathematical, systems, and design thinking, but multidimensional thinking is often ignored.

**Post 670 (01/04/25) - The Pull Process (Self-Learning)**
Learning is a "push-pull" process. External sources (teachers/books) push data, but a transformation happens when internal thinking pulls knowledge. This is *Swadhyaya*. Most children find what is taught irrelevant because teaching ignores the "why." Judgmental capabilities are at a higher level than reasoning—they provide solutions and compromises.

**Post 672 (03/04/25) - Intellectualism vs. Mechanical Life**
Mechanical life (job, family, stability) is risky in today's VUCA world. Routine clerical jobs are gone. Intellectualism means anticipating problems before they occur—a life skill. We should learn to "create value within change" rather than seek stability. Life itself is the biggest teacher.

**Post 673 (04/04/25) - Designing Compliant Workers**
American public education was "scientifically designed not to overeducate" but to produce socially compliant workers (W.T. Harris, 1880s). Macaulay followed this in India to produce non-thinking clerks. Edison noticed that between ages 11-15, children suffer from an "atrophy of curiosity" due to schools. We must shift to open, inquisitive, and deep thinking.

**Post 674 (05/04/25) - The Essence of Trust**
Trust is a part of your mindset; it builds relationships and supports life. Businesses (and IT services like TCS) run on trust. I succeeded because of trust established with colleges, governments, and industries. Sincerity and judgment capacity are prerequisites. One-sided trust doesn't work.

### On Academic Freedom and Research Proofs

**Post 686 (17/04/25)**
Academic freedom is the freedom to do academic work without political/administrative control, based on collective peer group decisions.
1. Define research directions and policies.
2. Build curriculum and evaluation rules.
3. Peer reviews for furtherance of knowledge.
However, freedom is not a license to abuse. It is a judgmental process. In many Indian universities, politics (both Left and Right) dominates, destroying academic truth.

**Post 690 (21/04/25) - Validating Research**
Clarity evolves through modeling and analysis.
- **Publications**: Do they provide a proof? With vast areas and fast changes, the referee system is overstretched, leading to retractions.
- **Conferences**: Mostly disinterested audiences; not focused fact-finding missions.
- **PhD Viva**: At IISc, it is a serious peer review. But in many IITs and universities, evaluations are becoming a mere formality. Supervisors sometimes express displeasure at too many questions!
- **AI Evaluators**: Now AI is being used as an evaluator of evaluations, leading to more withdrawn publications.

### Institutional Governance and Ambience

**Post 694 (25/04/25) - Academic Freedom in India**
Historically, fear pervaded in most Indian institutions. Under the British, Indians were treated as slaves. Even at IISc (1909-1945), dominance by British professors meant Indian faculty were not equals. CV Raman, the first Indian director, was dismissed as a "traitor" for inviting Max Born.
True academic freedom needs:
1. **Just Selection Processes** for students and faculty.
2. **Participative Governance**: Transparent bodies like a Senate, Council, and Curriculum Committees.
A strong academic ambience reduces anxiety and fear, allowing for the free growth of scholarship.

**Post 697-698 (28/04/25 - 29/04/25) - Managing Change**
In a fast-changing world, we must become "Change Managers."
- **Institutional Lag**: IISc and IITs were often 10-20 years behind in adopting the Internet and PC revolutions. In the 70s, IISc professors argued that software was "not an engineering subject."
- **Predicative Outlook**: We need courses on "Change Management." We must predict social and technological transformations 5-10 years ahead.
- **Lessons from Others**: Markets are dynamic; stockbrokers are alert and nimble. Lawyers adapt to new cases daily. Politicians study people's feelings. We should train children to face uncertainties rather than pampering them.

**Post 710-711 (11/05/25 - 12/05/25) - Spiritual Infrastructure**
We need a "ministry of spiritual development," not just GDP growth.
- **Incentivize Values**: Law currently presupposes people are criminals. We need an incentive system for honesty and helping others.
- **Move Beyond Profit**: Industries should ignore profit-fixation and shareholder-primacy in favor of ethics and people's welfare.
- **New Economics**: Economists must rework models to include values and consciousness alongside monetary metrics.

**Post 716-717 (17/05/25 - 18/05/25) - The Concept of Ambience**
Ambience is a human ecosystem—a combination of work culture, freedom, and futuristic outlook. Infrastructure is secondary.
- **Visionaries**: Homi Bhabha was a true visionary. He didn't just plan research; he built a blueprint for atomic energy away from bureaucracy and established schools (BARC) to produce expertise. Satish Dhawan followed this practical, futuristic approach.
- **The Routine Trap**: Most directors in India do day-to-day routine administration. They lack vision, innovative thinking, and change management capabilities. Consequently, institutions languish.

### Institutional Ethos and Quality

**Post 727 (28/05/25) - Development of Bangalore's Industrial Base**
The arrival of public sector units like HAL, ITI, BEL, and HMT spurred growth. In 1978, Electronic City was established by KEONICS. Bangalore moved ahead of other cities due to its focus on electronics and aerospace. IISc played a significant role in this transformation, providing the intellectual backdrop and expert consultancy.

**Post 730 (31/05/25) - The Ambience of Excellence**
The IISc ambience is built on core principles:
1. **Freedom and Independence**: No fear of the unknown.
2. **Futuristic Assessment**: Looking outside (global trends) and anticipating change.
3. **Cross-fertilization**: Moving from 10 departments in 1965 to 40+ units today through informal grouping and dynamic restructuring.
4. **Decentralization**: Even a junior faculty can independently decide on their research and teaching paths.

**Post 731 (01/06/25) - Intrinsic Worth vs. External Show**
We often judge by appearance—a gold tumbler vs. a copper one—even if the milk is the same. Educational institutions attract students with infrastructure, not teaching quality. 
True indicators of quality:
- Transparency and participative governance.
- Mindset of the people (open vs. fixed).
- Institutional history and clarity on future directions.

**Post 735 (05/06/25) - The Role of Visionary Leadership**
Leadership falls into three categories:
1. **Administrators**: Focused on rules and control; loves bureaucracy. (This is the tragedy of most Indian leaders).
2. **Transformative**: Changes an institution or government through action (e.g., Ramakrishna Hegde).
3. **Visionary**: Builds futuristic units (e.g., Homi Bhabha, Satish Dhawan).
A leader should have *Viveka* (discriminatory power) and run a participative, transparent system.

**Post 737 (07/06/25) - The Rise of Administration**
While academics were initially smooth, the growth of a separate administration structure (initiated under SS Prabhu) created a huge, often inefficient bureaucracy. When a director is weak, administration overrides academics.
Furthermore, the physical expansion of the institute has become an "architectural chaos"—too many buildings, no soul or synergy in expansion, and poor drainage.

**Post 738-740 (08/06/25 - 10/06/25) - Resistance to Academic Change**
I encountered huge walls of resistance when suggesting curriculum changes. One professor said the syllabus "stood the test of time" despite being decades old.
- **Mental Retirement**: Most faculty "retire mentally" by age 35. They satisfy themselves with routine teaching and avoid new experiments.
- **Dependency**: A culture of waiting for initiatives to come from the "top" kills innovation.
- **Fear of Failure**: The system accepts only successes and doesn't value research that proves an approach doesn't work.

**Post 748-749 (18/06/25 - 19/06/25) - Systems Thinking in Engineering**
Power systems was the first area to conceive a "systems view"—modeling large interconnected networks with hundreds of nodes. While others focused on devices (machines), IISc taught power systems analysis, stability, and economics from a numerical and modeling standpoint. This interdisciplinary approach—combining computers, power systems, and numerical methods—was the future I embraced in 1966.

### The Future of Industry and Work

**Post 756 (26/06/25) - The Stagnation of Public Sector**
Many Indian industries failed (HEC Ranchi, HMT, ITI) because they were inward-looking and lived in yesterday's world. In contrast, TCS, Infosys, and HCL succeeded by anticipating future developments in a fast-growing IT area. Diversification and continuous improvement are the line between life and death for industries today.

**Post 760 (30/06/25) - Reskilling for the AI Age**
Traditional degrees (even MBAs) are becoming less relevant as AI transforms industries. By 2030, 92 million jobs may disappear while 170 million new ones are created. Lifelong learning is the only path. Professionals must dominate emotional intelligence and humanism where machines dominate logic.

**Post 765 (05/07/25) - Holistic Engineering Education**
Engineering education is currently too narrow. We need a shift toward "inclusive ecosystems" that include:
- **Social Engineering**: Understanding the human and digital economy.
- **Engineering Practices**: Moving beyond theory to application.
- **Impact Assessment**: Evaluating the quality of life, rural-urban divide, and environmental resources.

### Environmental Engineering and Resource Dynamics (1980s)

**Post 955 (14/01/26) - Cardinal Principles of Sustainability**
When we talk about the environment, we must address resource dynamics: accumulation, regeneration, and depletion.
1. Use mostly renewable resources.
2. Increase efficiency and reduce dependence on stored minerals (coal, petroleum).
3. Minimize waste through reuse and retrieval.
4. Increase natural storage (Afforestation, Biodiversity).

- **Firewood Logistics**: In the 1960s, Bangalore got its firewood from 60km away (Kanakapura). By 1980, it came from 500km away (Uttara Kannada) via eight train loads and 400 truck loads daily.
- **The Greenhouse Effect**: Increasing carbon accumulation traps heat, leading to erratic monsoons and rising sea levels which threaten Mumbai and Chennai.

### The Indian IT Revolution (1984–1994)

**Post 996-997 (24/02/26 - 25/02/26) - The Major Inflection Point**
The decade between 1984 and 1994 was the most critical for Indian computing.
- **Hardware Leap**: Computers moved from room-sized mainframes to desktops. Prices dropped from one crore (ten million) to 40,000 rupees. Companies like Eiko led with affordable models.
- **CPU Wars**: Intel (8086, 286, 386) and Motorola (68020, 68030) competed fiercely. I was one of the first in India to buy a Motorola 68020 system and upgrade it.
- **Software Landscape**: Unix was the giant step forward. C replaced FORTRAN and Pascal. Software engineering reached maturity with ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) and independent databases like Oracle and DB2.

**Post 998-999 (26/02/26 - 27/02/26) - Networking and Core Banking**
By 2000, India had a operational global email system (ERNET, operationalized by 1995) and the Internet.
- **Banking Transformation**: SBI's Core Banking project (designed and built by TCS) was a world-class achievement that proved India could handle complex, multidisciplinary IT systems.
- **Decentralization**: While others focused on centralized IT departments, we in the Karnataka Government took a different path in 1987. We empowered individual departments to manage their own computerization, which led to the success of Commercial Taxes and Excise automation.
