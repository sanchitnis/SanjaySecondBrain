# 📜 Memoirs of Prof. D.K. Subramanian

This page contains a chronological record of life events, career highlights, and historical anecdotes shared by DKS.

---

### Personal History & House Construction (1982-1984)

**Post 378**
Early 1980s were interesting and also difficult years for me. My house construction started in middle of 1982. It was a time of scarcity - both cement and steel were not available easily. Sand was also not available.

**Post 379**
I started construction of my house in Malleswaram in 1982. The first major item was Bhoomi Puja. I had no idea about it. My father suggested a good day and time. We went to the site and performed puja.

**Post 415 (13/07/24)**
Let me return to 1983 to look at my other activities. My house construction was mostly completed by mid 1983 except for floor polishing, plastering and electrical and water fittings plus getting approvals. We decided to conduct the grihapravesam ceremony... I invited most of my colleagues in the department and a few others... My parents were present. Most of the visitors liked the house... My father left after lunch. I didn't know at that time that was to be his last visit to my house and Bangalore. He passed away the next year.

**Post 418 (17/07/24)**
...I had a problem at my home front. My father was diagnosed with angina... Before I could bring a taxi to take him to the hospital, he passed away. It was sudden. That was a shock. It was Ramanavami day... I returned after the completion of the thirteen day ceremonies and continued with my activities.

### Career at IISc & Professional Leadership (1984-1985)

**Post 415 (Cont.)**
I became the chair of the Bangalore chapter of the computer society of India. The housing situation in bangalore was getting worse. After the protest by faculty, institute started two sets of quarters D with two bedrooms and E with three bedrooms... By late 1984, I became eligible for an allotment of a quarter in the campus. I moved into IISc in December 1984.

**Post 416 (15/07/24)**
...Madhav Gadgil prepared a proposal for setting up the centre of ecological sciences. I was another signatory to the proposal... in 1983, the centre was set up and I associated myself with the centre.

**Post 417 (16/07/24)**
...Let me continue with our movement to IISc quarters. We were allotted E20... life was heavenly. Children had lots of advantages... we were no longer known as the school of automation. From then on we became CSA... I chaired another committee to introduce a BE computer science program. We successfully started the BE program.

**Post 421 (20/07/24)**
During our international conference, I met Prof Santana Krishnan from TCE Madurai, my Alma mater... After that my visit to TCE became regular... I have been with their academic council for decades.

**Post 422 (21/07/24)**
...My next assignment was teaching a course at IIM Bangalore... managing to teach for about 5 years regularly in IIM Bangalore.

**Post 423 (22/07/24)**
...DRDO came up with a scheme of getting young BSc passed graduates and giving them a specialized two year MSc program in computer science... I was given a special visiting professor status at MK university.

### International Missions & Global Exposure (1985)

**Post 450 (18/08/24)**
Let me talk about my next international trip [1985]... I was scheduled to go to Tokyo for a conference and a meeting of TC7. CSI made me an Indian representative in IFIP TC7... He [the travel agent] recommended travel by Thai airways... I could extend my travel and go to Los Angeles on the same fare... I didn't know where I would stay in LA and what I would do.

**Post 451 (19/08/24)**
...Srivatsan helped me with his offer that I can stay with him. I wrote to Prof Setty at Asian Institute of Technology for a lecture... they offered to take care of my stay in their guest house during my visit to Bangkok. I wrote to National University, Singapore and they arranged a lecture... to UC Berkeley... my plate was full.

**Post 452 (20/08/24)**
...The travel from Tokyo to LA went across the Pacific ocean... 12 hours non stop... Srivatsan was waiting at the gate itself... [Later] I escaped mugging [in downtown LA].

**Post 453 (21/08/24)**
...Berkeley... I stayed in the guest house. After the talk at the environmental and resources department... I met the developers of Unix Berkeley version - Ben Thompson and Bill Joy - and Ingress data base. I met Michael Stonebreaker, who developed Ingress relational database, the first RDB in 1973.

**Post 454 (22/08/24)**
Now it was time to start the return leg... While checking in for Singapore flight... the person wanted to know whether I had 500 dollars as per Singapore government regulations. I said I don't have. But I have a ticket to India from there after a day. He issued the boarding pass.

**Post 454 (Cont.)**
...I reached Madras... I found out in the morning that the suitcase was not mine but a similar one. I went back to the airport... The other person demanded money. I gave him 200 rupees and returned to Bangalore by train. My children received their school backpacks.

### Family Support & Personal Health Challenge (1986-1987)

**Post 447 (15/08/24)**
...There is a woman behind every man... I got support from many women and men. The first was my grandmother who brought me up till I was 11 years old... during a long typhoid illness of about two months when I was in 10th standard... I acknowledge the support I received from my wife, daughter, son, parents and sister.

**Post 465 (02/09/24)**
The year 1986-1987... revealed my heart problem. I taught at NMAMIT, Nitte... One day after lunch, I climbed the hill to see the tall Gomateshwara. On return, I felt a chest pain, but ignored it and gave lectures for two hours. The pain persisted... Afterwards, I realised that God saved me that day.

**Post 465 (Cont.)**
I decided to see Dr Ramesh, a good cardiologist in bangalore. He did a stress test and told me that it showed a problem with my heart... The angio was done the next day at the National hospital [Bombay]... a block in the right ventricular artery, but I don't need a surgery. He prescribed medicines which I continue till date.

**Post 466 (03/09/24)**
[1987]... I was selected to go to AIT Bangkok to teach a course on decision support systems during the summer... It was a great experience... Library was very good... A few aspects of Thailand impressed me: Disciplined work culture, technical manuals in Thai language, Buddhist piety...

### Professional Trials and Transformations (1987–1996)

**Post 497 (04/10/24)**
Once I am committed to a task, I want to get it completed successfully... I felt that if I don't put myself into it, a project can become a rudderless ship. So I was a bit strong in my opinions. But I had an open mind but with a goal in focus... My popularity has been low because of that strong quality. But I needed it to succeed in a task.

**Post 497 (Cont.) - Work with Banks**
I got great support and respect from many EDs and GMs of SBI, Canara bank, Indian bank, Corporation bank, PNB, Syndicate bank, Vijaya bank and Equitas. I did a technology audit for SBI. Later I advised Canara bank on its first branch computerization project for its parliament street branch... The core banking transformation was not easy and the problems were different for each bank. My work with banks was my fourth dimension - the other three being academics, government and industries.

**Post 498 (05/10/24)**
I consider my involvements with many projects - in IISc, government of Karnataka, Mahithi sindhu, KSEEB, KSCST, APMC, Gadag, TCS, many banks, Central silk board, Jindal Steels, Jayadeva institute of Cardiology, Vidhana soudha/ PWD... land army corporation, DST... JNCASR, ISEC, DG shipping, IDRBT... very useful, very satisfying. These associations spanned for more than 40 years... I worked for more than 20 years post retirement from IISc.

### The IISc Power System Incidents

**Post 509 (16/10/24)**
After the inauguration of our 66kv substation in 1987... we decided to handover maintenance to BSES... Mr Gopal Rao was engineer in charge... unfortunate operator in the substation was not aware of maintenance work. So he switched on the 11 kv power supply on that line by mistake and a disaster struck. Both electricians working on the pole were electrocuted... Prof CNR and I were apprised... It brought bad publicity.

**Post 509 (Cont.) - Transformer Failure**
Within 18 months, [the transformer] failed. It was under warranty. So Cromptons had a legal obligation to repair it. But they refused. I visited Cromptons and met the vice-president to resolve the payment terms... I showed him the test certificate [with a rider]. He was convinced and waived the cost of repair.

**Post 510 (17/10/24)**
Secondly, I decided to have a second transformer so that continuity of supply can be ensured... I had to convince Prof CNR about it. In the end he agreed... The substation has been working satisfactorily till now.

**Post 513 (20/10/24)**

I agreed and visited BEL. I met Mr Rangarajan (Sujatha)... I gave a press conference rebutting the claims... I said “this EVM is not a PC. It is a standalone machine. It can’t be programmed. **Post 545 (21/11/24)**
The nineties decade was an important part of my life. My productivity and intellectual life went up. It was both a tough and highly satisfying period... I became the Dean of the engineering faculty by 1999. My role with professional societies became minimal.

**Post 554 (30/11/24) - The 1991 Unrest**
The year 1991 didn't start well. There was a massive unrest and violence in Bangalore... Mob violence against Tamils in Bengaluru and Mysore occurred on December 12–13, 1991... The national public school of Mr Gopalakrishnan was destroyed. All computers were broken. But he was and is a remarkable person and rebuilt the school.

**Post 545 (Cont.) - Major Projects**
I got involved in a big way with major IT transformations: mahithi sindhu program... total IT design for Jindal Vijayanagar steels, electrical and IT designs for sri Jayadeva institute of cardiology... an ERP system implementation called COWAA for the department of space... my involvement with the big NRDMS project at both KSCST level and at DST government of India level... indigenous GIS called GRAM development by Mrs Venkatachalam at IIT Bombay.

### Professional Consultancies and Transformations

**Post 559 (05/12/24) - Jayadeva Project**
Victoria hospital [space] given to Jayadeva was not adequate. So the government decided to set up Jayadeva Institute in a spacious place... I started with looking at the substation design first... At that time, the PWD department... had a problem with elevators. When Shankar Dayal Sharma, President of India, visited Bangalore, he got stuck in the elevator in the Raj bhavan. I recommended going for a hydraulic elevator... Later PWD installed a hydraulic elevator in the high court.

**Post 560 (06/12/24) - Central Silk Board**
The one major holistic activity that I was involved in during the late eighties and early nineties was the starting of computer activities in the central silk board... We identified ten functions... The project was given to CMC Ltd., a Government company. I had to get involved in all decisions... I helped the board in the purchase of computers.

**Post 561 (07/12/24) - Jindal Vijayanagar Steels (JVSL)**
I later met Mr Uday Singh, their boss... Discussions with Uday Singh went beyond IT. We considered SAP next. But we were not sure about getting talented people to run it... we found that it [SAP] doesn't meet any functions of the steel industry. We found out that USIT had integrated their PPC with oracle applications. So we decided on that. integration with PPC was established by a steel company - US steels. The hardware selection was easy; we chose the VAX machine from digital.

**Post 562 (08/12/24) - COWAA (ERP for Dept of Space)**
It was intended to build an ERP for the entire department of space... Dr Madhavan Nair talked to me about the feasibility of an inhouse development... I told him, “yes. It is possible... but it needs leadership.”

**Post 568 (14/12/24) - COWAA Execution**
We decided to meet once a month physically at SHAR, Sriharikota. So I visited SHAR once every month... This went on for three plus years... When more than 90% was complete, the teams lost interest, work slowed down. So they weren't keen on installing the software... It was run and was found useful in resolving issues of uniformity.

### Transforming Engineering Education (TEQIP)

**Post 569 (15/12/24) - Governance Principles**
The DoS Project revealed some important components for successful implementation... IT governance was misunderstood by most IT people, but this project employed many good governance principles... starting points like well articulated goal and objectives, organised teams, detailed plans... collective understanding, decision making, good and quick conflict resolutions.

**Post 570 (16/12/24) - TEQIP (World Bank Program)**
TEQIP was started in 2002... About 180 institutions were selected... In Karnataka, all aided institutions and two government/university colleges were chosen. In addition, about six private colleges were selected... The main focus was research... social and community aspects were also encouraged. SDM, Dharwad, conducted a welding course to jail inmates.

**Post 594 (09/01/25) - The Battle for Autonomy**
The states of engineering education in Karnataka at the beginning of TEQIP... no college had autonomy. Universities didn't want to lose control... a bitter fight was on the cards. Mr Kausik Mukerjee, secretary education, resolved this by piloting a special bill in the assembly... I developed a complete blue book on curriculum—stressing on flexibility, balancing, credits.

### The Mid-Century Engineering Landscape (1950s–1980s)

**Post 571 (17/12/24)**
Our engineering education was marginal till the fifties... The second five year plan focused on setting up several engineering colleges to provide a supply of engineers to state PWDs... The central government took an impactful step in the sixties—to set up IITs with foreign collaboration... IITs became beacons for our engineering education standards, though somewhat misleading from reality.

**Post 572 (18/12/24) - The Electronics Explosion**
The mid sixties saw the arrival of a major, highly impacting and new area—electronics... IISc was the first to set up a communications engineering department in 1948... The eighties saw the next explosion with the arrival of computers, PCs, programming, software, algorithms and data processing.

### Reflections on the Late 20th Century Transitions

**Post 643 (05/03/25)**
Let me sum up the situation till 1995.
1. **Agriculture**: We reached self-sufficiency in food grains by 1980 through the Green Revolution... thanks to ICAR researchers.
2. **Education Infrastructure**: Technical education gained big growth in the nineties with private colleges bridging the technology gap and allowing sub-disciplines like computer software and IT to thrive.
3. **Trade Restrictions**: Stagnation till 1990 due to wrong policies. IISc, Dept of Space, and DRDO faced technology restrictions from the US—could not even buy supercomputers or Oracle databases.
4. **Power Projects**: Displaced people with meagre compensation; affected forest ecology (desertification). Villages in hydro-power rich districts like Shimoga didn't have electricity till the nineties.
5. **EVM Defense**: The electronic voting machine, a great innovation, was opposed in the late 80s/early 90s by Mr VP Singh. I had to defend it to the press. Rangarajan (writer Sujatha) was in charge at BEL.
6. **Infrastructure Failures**: The freight scheduling system for railways was scuttled by George Fernandez in the late eighties. I was involved with that project; it didn't see progress for decades.

**Post 643 (Cont.)**
The nineties saw the emergence of IT services. We took leadership, and the economy was fueled by the private sector. By 2000, India's competency was recognized globally. Socialism as a concept died by the nineties—all parties had rich candidates and corruption flourished.

### Global Training and Educational Impact

**Post 646 (08/03/25) - EQUATE and Teacher Training**
From 2002 to 2020, I was incredibly busy... dean at IISc, NRDMS projects, World Bank state of environment coordinator... IT transformations in banks (Indian Bank, PNB, Corporation Bank, etc.). Still, I lectured in training programs under EQUATE (with Dr. Mathur).
I loved my talks on research methodology—definitions, processes, selection of topics. I vividly remember the talk at Jaipur—discussing discriminations like thermodynamics vs program vs thread. These experiences are now crystallized into a full-fledged *Transformation Engineering* course.

**Post 650 (12/03/25) - Mahithisindhu Success**
In the Mahithisindhu program, we identified difficult topics in math and science for class 8-10. I guided a company to effectively use video media. We distributed CDs to 1000 high schools. The outcome: pass percentage in SSLC jumped from 50% to 75% in five years.

### Childhood Roots and the 1952 Turning Point

**Post 675 (05/04/25) - Living in the Village**
I lived in a village for the first ten years of my life... simple, spartan, frugal life. It was a period of pre-independence and just after independence... in the care of my grandmother mostly. But my elder uncle took care of my learnings. He was my mentor and played a big role in my upbringing.
The village atmosphere was highly motivated by the fight for independence and idealistic to build the nation... I first learnt that routineness in daily activities—getting up at 5 am—is very important. I understood the "don'ts" even if not the logic—the advantages and benefits.

**Post 676 (07/04/25) - Discipline and Order**
Childhood is a high-speed learning of many activities... Upto the first ten years, we learn regularity, respect, piety, concentration, freedom, and responsibility. My grandmother and uncle never told me to obey what they say; they knew me well. But my parents wanted obedience.
I understood that discipline means *order*—Rig Veda's *Rthm*. Order is ensured through following a set of rules, an organized structure, and empowerment. I got empowerment at a young age of 7 years itself.

**Post 677-679 (08/04/25 - 10/04/25) - Learning through Observation**
- **The Marriage Project**: Organizing a marriage was a year-long planning activity—purchasing dresses (no readymades), license for sugar, cleaning, pandal, cooks, and meticulous account maintenance in a ledger.
- **Agriculture**: From age 7, I observed harvesting—enriching fields, plowing, planting, and the hectic coordination of thrashing and winnowing paddy. I learnt cultivation processes and market rates.
- **Legal/Negotiation**: My uncle was consulted for legal matters. I observed him drafting sale deeds, promissory notes, and mediating property distributions. I saw the pain of my father and uncles during my grandfather's property settlement.
- **Building Materials**: At age 8, I watched house construction—using lime and sand (cement was unavailable), grinding with bullocks, and using shell lime for whitewashing.

**Post 680 (11/04/25) - The 1952 Turning Point**
1952 was the first turning point in my life. The usual village boy's plan was to study upto 8th, learn typing/shorthand, and migrate for a job. But my uncle made a momentous decision to move me to Tirunelveli for high school. This opened up opportunities for higher education. I almost decided at that young age of 10 years on my goal—to become an engineer.

**Post 681-685 (12/04/25 - 16/04/25) - City Schooling and Reading**
- **City vs Village**: Social interactions shifted from high (village) to minimal (city). I moved from a production-based environment to a market-based one.
- **Voracious Reading**: I read a book every week—Tamil fiction (Kalki), biographies (U.V. Swaminatha Iyer, Yogacharya Sundaram). I read weekly magazines like *Alaiosai* (classic informative writing).
- **Politics**: Became aware of politics in the 50s—Rajaji's rule, the 1953 Andhra reorganization, and the rise of the DMK (Anti-Hindi/Anti-Brahmin agitations).
- **Teachers**: I speciallly remember Mr. R. Ramaswamy for atomic physics and Mr. K.K. Menon for a solid foundation in mathematics (calculus and matrices).
- **Stories**: My knowledge of Mahabharata came from Rajaji's *Vyasar Virundhu*. The story of Arjuna's focus (the bird's eye) stayed with me; I applied it to concentration and memory.

### Influencers and the Road to Engineering

**Post 699 (30/04/25) - Grandfather's Influence**
My grandfather was a scholar in Tamil, Malayalam, and Sanskrit. He was a great orator who delivered lectures on sastras and ithihasas. He led a stable and very frugal life... Even though the time I spent with him was short, it gave me an appreciation of simple living and the effort needed to improve cognition. He even influenced freedom fighters like Mr. Dikshitar (MLA) to join the struggle through his talks.

**Post 700 (01/05/25) - Palayamkottai: English Medium and Classics**
In 1958-59, I moved to St. Xavier's College for PUC. I became independent, managing the household and my sister's schooling at 17. The medium of education shifted to English. I realized my potential in Maths—getting 100% in all tests.
- **British Classics**: My English teacher introduced me to Boswell, Johnson, Dickens, and Mark Twain. I cultivated a taste for English villages and London through Sherlock Holmes and *Great Expectations*.
- **Tamil Roots**: Kalki's novels taught me Tamil history and the independence struggle. Sundaram's biography introduced me to yoga and the idea of Ulsoor/Bangalore.

**Post 701 (02/05/25) - Yogacharya Sundaram**
He was a yoga teacher in the 40s/50s in Ulsoor. I learnt Pranayama from his book. Another influence was Swami Sivananda, who gave my father the title "Pravachana Ratna". These sources opened worlds of yoga and proper living for me.

### First Steps in the "Tata Institute" (1965)

**Post 712-713 (13/05/25 - 14/05/25) - Landing in Bangalore**
I got disillusioned with my first industrial job in Madras and applied to IISc again. I landed in Bangalore on July 28, 1965. My SR number was the first for that year.
- **First Impressions**: Bangalore Railway Station was small and village-like. But the weather was cool and people were polite. Locals called IISc "Tata Institute" (pronounced *Thatha*—grandfather—institute).
- **The Campus**: I saw broad roads, huge circles, and lots of trees. There was no gate or signboard then. The main building's tower was the landmark.

**Post 714-715 (15/05/25 - 16/05/25) - Day One and The Library**
- **Admissions**: A smooth, offline process. I had six copies of the receipt for the hostel, department, library, health centre, and gymkhana.
- **Hostel**: I was allotted H4 in H block (the northern end then). I ate at A-mess; the food was sumptuous, and the Sunday Masala Dosa was a highlight.
- **The Library**: An amazing experience. I used the textbook reservation facility (one-hour slots) and attended the Friday journal display. The library was the main source of research updates in the pre-internet era.

**Post 718-720 (19/05/25 - 21/05/25) - Academic Rigor**
Classes began on August 2. We were a diverse group of seven students from different states.
- **Adjustment**: It was a "run from day one" system with continuous assessments and surprise quizzes. Many struggled, but I was at ease, auditing extra courses like German and Control Systems.
- **Finances**: My total monthly expenses for room and food were just 100 rupees. I felt financially well-off!

### Nostalgia of Old Bangalore (1965-70)

**Post 722 (23/05/25) - A City of Contrasts**
Bangalore was spread out, unlike Chennai or Kolkata.
- **Malleswaram**: Broad roads, tree-lined avenues (Sampige and Margosa), and bungalow houses with jackfruit trees. Minimal traffic; I could walk freely in the evenings.
- **Yeshwantpur**: Crowded, narrow streets, and small houses—a sharp contrast just across from IISc.
- **Divided City**: The "pete" (Basavanagudi/Malleswaram) was Mysore state-governed, while the Cantonment was under the British/Madras administration.
- **The Lakes**: Bangalore had about 160 lakes then. Dharmambudhi was a maidan in 1965 (now City Bus Stand). Kempambudhi (now a sports complex) and Mathikere were also lost to urbanization.

**Post 725 (26/05/25) - The Transport Struggle**
- **Madurai (TVS)**: The ideal system—punctual, dynamic scheduling, and integrated management.
- **Calcutta/Delhi/Madras**: Each had functional (if crowded) modes like trams, minibuses, or suburban trains.
- **Bangalore (BTS)**: A total disappointment. Erratic frequencies and terrible buses. Route 11 (Malleswaram to Gandhi Bazar) was the only reliable one. For computer work at NAL or HAL, travel was an arduous task.

**Post 728 (29/05/25) - Food and Coffee Culture**
- **Coffee by Two**: A unique Bangalore practice of sharing one coffee in two tumblers.
- **Iyengar Bakeries**: A local invention, popular for bun-butter-jam and badam milk.
- **MTR**: Famous for rava idly; the queue system and open kitchen were an experience even then.

**Post 729 (30/05/25) - IISc Facilities**
The institute had world-class facilities: the AC network analyser (used to design India's power grids), electron microscopes, wind tunnels, and hydraulics labs. This global connect brought experts like Kalman, Sana, and Kailath for lectures.

### The Researcher's Path (1966-67)

**Post 741 (11/06/25) - The First Term Paper**
In Jan-April 1966, I wrote a term paper on "Digital Transducers" for Prof KS Prabhu. I spent 60 hours reading 25+ papers and synthesizing a review. This boosted my confidence and removed my fear of the unknown.

**Post 742-743 (12/06/25 - 13/06/25) - Choosing Computer Science**
I rejected industry jobs after a boring 11-month stint at Easun (Madras). Under Dr. Dharma Rao, I took up a project on "Power Systems Stability" using Lyapunov models. I audited control systems courses and eventually shifted to computer-based numerical solutions for load flow problems.

**Post 744 (14/06/25) - The Ferranti Sirius Computer**
IISc had no computer in 1966. I used NAL's Ferranti Sirius computer (Belur campus).
- **Logistics**: Changing two buses and traveling two hours each way.
- **Funding**: Prof HNR secured a 10,000 rupee grant from CBIP, which covered my computer hire charges.
- **Programming**: Paper tape input, shift registers, and a 4K word memory. I wrote my first 10-line program, debugged it, and finally solved a 40-node power grid problem by Feb 1967.

**Post 745 (15/06/25) - Joining for PhD**
On August 1, 1967, I joined as a research student under Prof HNR. I received a UGC senior fellowship. My ME scholarship ended the day before, and the new one started without a gap.

### Transition to Faculty (1968-1970)

**Post 751 (21/06/25) - Thesis Submission**
I submitted my PhD thesis in May 1970. My research focused on gap analysis in power systems—specifically, moving from linear approximations to nonlinear models for economic dispatch and dynamics. I also explored digital control architecture, which was just emerging as a transformation over analog/manual systems.

**Post 752-753 (22/06/25 - 23/06/25) - Becoming a Lecturer**
I was selected as a Lecturer in 1968. The selection committee included Prof MV Hariharan (IIT Bombay) and Prof Ganapathy (Warangal). 
- **The Russian Connect**: Post-Tashkent agreement, USSR helped set up the Advanced School of Automation at IISc—a marriage of computers and control.
- **Teaching Load**: While still a research student, I taught Electrical Machines, Drawing, and Stability (covering for Prof HNR during his Moscow visit). 
- **Course Reform**: I shifted the "Computer Techniques" course from analog (8-10 lectures) to digital principles (35 hours), using a Burroughs Corporation staff manual as a text.

**Post 754 (24/06/25) - Assistant Warden Duty**
Prof AR Vasudevamurthy invited me to be an Assistant Warden. I took charge of:
- **Hostel Modernization**: Streamlined room allotment using a correct database, and introduced steam cooking in the kitchen to abolish firewood and reduce mess bills. 
- **Staff Restructuring**: Created a proper hierarchy (Head Cook, Cook, Supervisor) for mess employees, who were permanent institute staff.

**Post 755 (25/06/25) - Improving Institutional Infrastructure**
- **IISc Power Grid**: From 1968 to 2004, I led several major changes: moving from a single 11kv substation to a 66kv/11kv ring main system with 4MW diesel backup. 
- **Administrative Reform**: Streamlined procedures for staff evaluation, ASTRA, and DST, focusing on minimizing administrative time while maximizing efficiency.

**Post 771-772 (11/07/25 - 12/07/25) - Academic Reforms**
- **Entrance Exams**: In 1969, I helped conduct the institute's first entrance tests for BE and ME admissions in Bangalore, Chennai, and Mumbai.
- **Restructured Labs**: I redesigned the Electrical Machines lab to focus on design and deeper learning rather than ritualistic experiments. We abolished formal exams, replacing them with continuous evaluation and a rigorous 15-minute viva.
- **Credit System**: I served on the curriculum committee under Prof BS Ramakrishna. We designed the 5-grade (A-F) system and later the 7-grade (S-F) system, making IISc the first in India to introduce such flexibility.

**Post 774 (14/07/25) - Values in Management**
I encountered several ethical challenges as a warden. Many students stayed unofficially after graduation or left without paying mess dues. In the mid-80s, I refused to help a former student (then at TCS) get his degree certificate until he cleared his decade-old mess dues, emphasizing that "values are more important than convenience."

### Expansion and Travels (1969-1970)

**Post 779 (20/07/25) - Hostel Infrastructure**
I stayed in F66 in the hostel until the end of 1970. During this period, Prof. Dhawan took a personal interest in constructing new blocks: C Mess, married student apartments, and a huge gymkhana. Based on an energy audit report I submitted, we shifted from energy-guzzling boilers to geysers in all hostel bathrooms—a practice followed in all subsequent constructions.

**Post 781-782 (21/07/25 - 22/07/25) - First Visit to Bombay (1969)**
I needed the CDC3600 computer at TIFR for my PhD and stayed in the Colaba atomic energy hostel for 15 days. 
- **The Journey**: Leisurley travel via Miraj/Pune instead of Guntakal.
- **Computing**: I picked up Fortran in one day at TIFR. It was a dream period of computing—batch systems were well-organized, and the high-speed machine made debugging fast compared to the low-level languages used at HAL/NAL.
- **Ambience**: TIFR was an oasis—clean, serene, and serious. I met Dr. Sarabhai, N. Seshagiri, and Prof R. Narasimhan.

**Post 790-791 (30/07/25 - 31/07/25) - Networking and Career**
- **Admissions**: In 1970, I helped conduct the first common entrance exam for BE admissions in the EE, ECE, and ME departments.
- **Teaching**: From August 1970, I taught mathematics to biochemistry students for three years, covering for Mr. R. Vaidyanathan.
- **Travels**: 1969 was a year of travels: Madras (calculator repairs), Ahmedabad (witnessing the moon landing on TV), Delhi, and my first visit to IIT Kanpur in the biting cold.
- **Thesis Completion**: After Prof HNR left for UPSC, I chose Prof Deekshatulu and later Prof Iyengar as supervisors. I submitted my thesis in April 1970.

**Post 793 (02/08/25) - Research Philosophy**
I chose a lonely path: Power Systems Analysis + Numerical Methods + Computer Programming. At the time, many IISc faculty considered programming "inferior" and software "not a discipline." I didn't get scared of being a rebel; I got the courage to be different, solving large problems that analytical methods couldn't handle.

### Professional Growth and Institutional Responsibility (1971-1973)

**Post 818 (27/08/25) - Datamatics and Programming**
In mid-1970, I started teaching Fortran at Datamatics Corporation (founded by Dr. LS Kanodia). I picked up this second job on Prof. Devanathan's recommendation. Lectures were in the evenings at the Bank of India building, KG Road. This association continued until 1978 and allowed me to dig deep into programming languages (Fortran, Autocoder, COBOL) while training hundreds of students and industry experts.

**Post 818-819 (27/08/25 - 28/08/25) - Time Table Committee**
In 1971, Prof. Dhawan appointed me Convenor of the Time Table Committee. I built a computer algorithm to automate the process, which impressed Prof. R. Narasimha. Despite being a junior lecturer, I was entrusted with the complex task of scheduling all courses in the new central lecture hall complex. I had some "run-ins" with senior faculty over room allocations, but the experience was my first successful job as a leader.

**Post 842-843 (20/09/25 - 21/09/25) - The Move to School of Automation (1973)**
By early 1973, I was promoted to Assistant Professor. It was a major shift:
- **Promotion**: I joined the newly formed School of Automation (SA) full-time. 
- **The Context**: Although the scope of SA aligned with my computer science goals, the transition was tough. SA’s chair, Prof. IGS, had a non-cordial relationship with the EE department. I performed a delicate balancing act, continuing to teach EE courses for the next five years.
- **Housing**: I moved from the hostel to a rented house in Malleswaram 11th main (near Cluny Convent). I stayed there for 12 years (until 1985).

**Post 844 (22/09/25) - Five-Year Review**
Prof. Dhawan introduced the five-year review system in 1967. It was an excellent motivating mechanism that rewarded merit and encouraged faculty to think ahead about their research, teaching, and social commitments every five years.

**Post 848 (26/09/25) - Commonwealth Scholarship (1973)**
In April 1973, I was selected for a Commonwealth Scholarship at the University of Manchester. Prof. Dhawan recommended my name without me even applying. I chose Operating Systems and Scheduling as my research area. Though the scholarship started in Sept 1973, I delayed my departure to January 1974 to complete my teaching commitments.

**Post 848 (26/09/25) - Marriage (1973)**
My marriage was conducted in a hurry in September 1973 in Thiruttani. My grandmother was upset that I was going to England alone, so I had to promise to marry before leaving. We set up our first home with furniture (cots and almirahs) provided via my friend Chandrasekhara at Datamatics.

### The Manchester Year (1974)

**Post 851-852 (01/10/25 - 02/10/25) - Life in Manchester**
1974 was a year of contrasts. England was in the grip of a severe coal strike, leading to a three-day week and major energy shortages. I saw the fall of the Heath government and the rise of Harold Wilson’s Labour. Despite the gloom, the NHS was impressive; my wife and I were attached to a neighborhood doctor, and welfare support for nutrition was excellent.

**Post 856 (04/10/25) - Habits and Hobbies**
In the UK, I developed a "TV addiction" (channels were limited to BBC1, BBC2, and ITV). I loved watching *The Saint* (Roger Moore) and science programs that simplified complex phenomena. I also picked up an interest in Jazz and read extensively from the public libraries—journals like *Which?* for product reviews and ACM computing surveys to stay at the cutting edge of software methodology.

**Post 857-858 (05/10/25 - 06/10/25) - Travel and Photography**
We traveled to the Lake District (inspired by Wordsworth), Liverpool, and London (Buckingham Palace, 221B Baker St, and Madame Tussauds). I bought a Zenith SLR camera and started taking hundreds of color slides, which became a permanent documentation of our lives.

**Post 858-859 (06/10/25 - 07/10/25) - UMIST Association**
I spent part of my time at the Control Systems Centre at UMIST, collaborating with Dr. Barney. I guided two MS students and learned a disciplined method of project management where students recorded action points in notebooks—a practice I later saw mirrored by successful leaders like Dr. Nirmal Jain of TCS.

### Return to India and New Challenges (1975)

**Post 860 (08/10/25) - Resettling at IISc**
I returned to Bangalore in February 1975. The customs experience in Bombay was "terrible," but Prof. Dhawan’s personal concern upon my return was overwhelming. Since my house in Malleswaram 11th main was occupied, I stayed in the hostel guest room.
In August 1975, I began teaching three new courses in the School of Automation: Data Structures, Systems Programming, and Operating Systems. These became my staples for the next two decades.

**Post 872 (21/10/25) - Personal Milestones**
My daughter was born in March 1975 in Chengalpet. Later that year, we finally moved into our Malleswaram house. After a brief stay at Prof. Krishnan’s house in Palace Orchards (which I called "an upper-class slum" due to the noise), we returned to 11th Main.

**Post 874-875 (23/10/25 - 24/10/25) - The Emergency (1975)**
The political situation deteriorated with the declaration of Emergency. Fear was palpable. My friend R. Ramalingam (IPS) was in a tense position having arrested senior opposition leaders like Vajpayee and Advani. Interestingly, during this period of high risk, I invested my sabbatical savings in a site in West of Chord Road, purchased from Byrappaji, an honest MLA who needed funds.

### Diversification and State Policy (1975–1976)

**Post 899 (17/11/25) - From Electrical Engineer to Generic Problem Solver**
The years 1975 and 76 opened my mind. I broke the mental barrier that I was just an electrical engineer. I got involved in water supply, power distribution (Jharia project), and energy systems. My work with KSCST introduced me to the Karnataka government—meeting Prof. Dhawan, Ghorpade (Finance Minister), and GVK Rao (Chief Secretary). This was the start of three decades of helping the state with its infrastructure.

**Post 905 (24/11/25) - Personal Life**
My son was born in December 1976 in Mysore. Meanwhile, Bangalore was growing faster than its infrastructure, leading to the first major power cuts and the need for IISc to install diesel generators. I worked with KEB's chief engineer, BG Rudrappa, to secure some relief for the Institute.

### The Delhi Power Study (1976–1977)

**Post 906-907 (25/11/25 - 26/11/25) - Revamping the Capital’s Grid**
In mid-1976, after a power outage at an event attended by Indira Gandhi, I was tasked with studying Delhi’s electrical distribution. It was ten times more complicated than Jharia. The system was archaic, protection was poor, and tariffs were frozen due to politics. I spent days inspecting the 33kV ring road line and Chandni Chowk’s chaotic LT distribution. By late 1977, I completed a standard design recommending 66kV for sub-transmission and automatic change-over switches to improve reliability.

### The Move to IIT Bombay (1977–1978)

**Post 912 (01/12/25) - Selection as Professor**
In late 1977, I was called for an interview at IIT Bombay. Prof. R. Narasimhan was the expert. I was selected as Professor, but my loyalty remained with IISc. I agreed to go on leave, seeing it as an opportunity to change my environment and escape the departmental politics of the School of Automation.

**Post 914-917 (03/12/25 - 06/12/25) - The Harvard/Smithsonian Trip (Jan 1978)**
Before joining IITB, I was invited to Harvard and the Smithsonian to discuss rural energy. It was a "stupid" travel schedule by today’s standards—JFK to Boston with only an hour's gap—but it worked. I arrived in a frozen Boston in January, shared our ASTRA experience on wood-burning stoves, and realized that my ground-level knowledge was highly valued internationally.

### Life at Powai (1978–1979)

**Post 918-920 (07/12/25 - 10/12/25) - Settling in Bombay**
I joined IIT Bombay in January 1978. Life in Bombay was a contrast to Bangalore: things moved fast. I got a gas connection and ration card in days without bribes. My son learned to cycle in our massive BTR quarters hall. I taught Operating Systems and designed a new M.Tech curriculum, but despite the comfort, I decided in May 1978 that I would return to IISc for ASTRA.

### Philosophy of Public Service

**Post 950 (09/01/26) - Early Foundations**
My societal journey began early, in the 1940s and 50s. We lived in public view, where societal bonds were strong. I learned the habit of service from my father and grandfather, who delivered lectures on Ramayana and provided psychological support to families in grief. My uncle, the principal of government college, further strengthened and organized this concept of service in me.

**Post 950 (09/01/26) - Service to IISc**
When I joined IISc as a faculty, I focused my public service on the Institute. For over 35 years, I served as the warden, chair of the power committee, and chair of the evaluation committee for staff promotions (for 14 years). This was free, empathetic service—I never expected payments. I also provided free programming support to outsiders in the 60s and 70s, as a way to give back for the help I received from NAL, HAL, and TIFR.

### Managing Health (1980–1987)

**Post 950-951 (09/01/26 - 10/01/26) - Diagnosing Diabetes**
In 1980, I was diagnosed as diabetic. I inherited this from my father and grandfather, so the news didn't shock me. I took it as a routine lifestyle change.
- **Management**: Regularity in food (soaked fenugreek seeds, avoiding sugar, oats for breakfast), walking, and medication.
- **Psychological Mastery**: A positive frame of mind is essential for a diabetic. Emotions affect sugar levels. I decided that my quality of life would remain the same, diabetes or not. Dr. GR Nagabhushan (IISc Health Centre) initially said I would live up to 70; I am surprised and happy to be much older now.

### Family and Education (1980–1981)

**Post 951 (10/01/26) - The Choice of KV IISc**
We chose Kendriya Vidyalaya (KV) IISc for our children. I preferred the KV curriculum over the state curriculum for its rigor, better science labs, and egalitarian culture. Unlike private schools, which built non-existent superiority complexes, KV produced socially strong students with good communication skills.

**Post 973 (02/02/26) - Family Travels and Exposure**
I took my family with me during my government visits to forest areas like Bandipur, Nagarhole, and Mercara. They experienced travel by crowded buses, and saw diverse parts of India—Simla, Delhi, and Kolkatta. My goal was to give them independence, freedom, and responsibility, similar to my own upbringing where I learned through observation and reading.

### Joining the State Government (1980–1981)

**Post 952-953 (11/01/26 - 12/01/26) - Additional Secretary (S&T and Ecology)**
In October 1980, under CM Gundu Rao, the Karnataka Government set up the departments of Science & Technology and Ecology & Environment. Despite initial reluctance, I accepted the position of Additional Secretary.
- **Balancing Act**: I kept my research and teaching at IISc, lecturing in the morning and working at Vidhanasoudha from 10 AM to 4:30 PM.
- **Motivation**: I wanted to understand "problem discovery" from the user and state angle, rather than just the abstract STEM angle. This role provided a massive field for practical application across departments—computerization, power systems, and environmental protection.

**Post 953 (12/01/26) - Honesty in Vidhanasoudha**
To my surprise, I found a high level of honesty among the IAS officers and staff in Vidhanasoudha. While ignorance and disinterest were common, many legislators and officers were simple, sincere people.

### Academic Progress and Reorganization (1980s)

**Post 991 (19/02/26) - Structuring CSE at IISc**
Upon returning from IIT Bombay, my plate filled fast. I structured and started the ME CSE and BE CSE programs in the department. We separated control systems into a new ME program called "System Science and Automation" (SSA), run jointly with the EE department. I taught courses with some of the highest registrations—Data Structures, Operating Systems, and Databases.

**Post 991, 1022-1023 (19/02/26 - 23/03/26) - Solving the Power Crisis**
IISc's power supply was failing as the 11KV station became overloaded by neighboring connections (BHEL, BWSSB).
- **The 66KV Solution**: In 1984, I proposed moving IISc to a 66KV supply from a new receiving station at Sanjay Nagar.
- **Strategic Pitch**: I convinced Director CNR Rao by explaining that internal 11KV augmentation made us one of 10,000 consumers, while a 66KV line made us one of 10. He gave the green signal, ensuring IISc's power stability for the next 30 years.

### Professional Leadership (1984–1985)

**Post 1015 (15/03/26) - CSI and IFIP**
In 1984, I took over as Chair of the Computer Society of India (CSI), Bangalore Chapter.
- **International Conference (1985)**: We organized a major IFIP conference on CAD/CAM/CAE for Industrial Progress at the Taj Hotel. It was a massive success with over 500 participants, despite the tragedy of the Air India Kanishka crash which forced us to replace a speaker from Canada.
- **Historical Context**: During a review committee visit in October 1984, we received news of PM Indira Gandhi's assassination. We had to cancel our planned CSI felicitation and ensure our guests (Prof. Nag and Prof. PVS Rao) returned home safely.

**Post 546 (22/11/24) - Education Reforms**
[With] Prof NR Shetty moving to Bangalore as VC, my involvement with them also increased. I popularized the starting of BCA and MCA in most colleges in karnataka by inspecting them... Towards the end of the decade, world Bank came up with a new program called TEQIP. I played a good role in that.
